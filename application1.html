Real-World Application: Political Deepfakes and Democratic Integrity

Written by: Nick Nguyen

Introduction: Deepfakes as a Threat to Democracy

Political deepfakes represent a transformative threat to democratic integrity by weaponizing artificial intelligence to erode the foundation of shared reality. Unlike traditional propaganda, deepfakes can fabricate hyper-realistic audio and video of world leaders, making them say or do things that never occurred. This technology creates a "liar's dividend," where the mere existence of deepfakes allows politicians to dismiss genuine, incriminating evidence as fake, while simultaneously making it nearly impossible for the average voter to distinguish between truth and fabrication. When the public can no longer trust their own eyes and ears, the resulting cynicism and confusion can suppress voter turnout, incite social unrest, and shift election outcomes before a correction can even be issued.

Case Study 1: The 2024 Biden Robocall Incident

In January 2024, just days before the New Hampshire primary, thousands of voters received an automated phone call featuring a voice that sounded identical to President Joe Biden. The voice used Biden’s signature phrase, "What a bunch of malarkey," and urged Democrats to stay home and "save your vote" for the general election in November, rather than participating in the primary. This was a sophisticated audio deepfake designed specifically for voter suppression.

The deepfake was later traced to a political consultant, Steve Kramer, who hired a magician to create the AI voice clone using software from ElevenLabs. The goal was to demonstrate the vulnerabilities of the electoral system, but the impact was an immediate threat to the democratic process. The audio was realistic enough to fool casual listeners, and because it was distributed via private phone calls rather than public social media, it was difficult for fact-checkers to detect and debunk in real-time.

The consequences were significant. The incident prompted the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) to officially outlaw AI-generated voices in robocalls under the Telephone Consumer Protection Act. While the deepfake did not ultimately flip the primary outcome, it served as a "canary in the coal mine" for the 2024 election cycle, highlighting how easily and cheaply a single actor can utilize AI to interfere with the voting behavior of thousands of citizens.

Case Study 2: The Zelenskyy Surrender Deepfake

In March 2022, during the early weeks of the Russian invasion of Ukraine, a video appeared on Ukrainian news websites and social media showing President Volodymyr Zelenskyy standing behind a podium and telling his soldiers to lay down their arms and surrender to Russian forces. The video was a "deepfake"—the first of its kind used in a high-stakes kinetic war to attempt to influence military morale and national resolve.

The video was created using GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks) to map Zelenskyy's face onto an actor’s body. While the quality was somewhat low—Zelenskyy’s head appeared slightly too large and his voice was deeper than usual—the video was spread via a hacked news ticker on the "Ukraine 24" channel. The goal was to create immediate chaos and trigger a mass surrender of Ukrainian troops during a critical defensive period.

The attempt failed largely due to a pre-emptive educational campaign by the Ukrainian government and Zelenskyy’s rapid response. Within minutes of the video’s spread, Zelenskyy posted a real video from the streets of Kyiv debunking the fake. However, the incident set a dangerous precedent for "information warfare," showing how deepfakes can be used as a weapon to bypass traditional diplomacy and military force to attack a nation's psychological will to fight.

Why These Situations Are Complex and Challenging

Combating political deepfakes is an uphill battle because of the "asymmetry of speed." A deepfake can be generated in minutes and go viral across global platforms in seconds, whereas professional fact-checking and technical verification can take hours or days. By the time a video is debunked, the initial emotional impact—anger, fear, or confirmation of a bias—has already taken root in the viewer's mind. Furthermore, confirmation bias plays a critical role; individuals are statistically less likely to question the authenticity of a video if it portrays a political opponent in a negative light, making disinformation self-propagating within digital echo chambers.

Technical detection also faces the "cat and mouse" problem. As detection algorithms improve, the AI models used to create deepfakes also improve by learning to bypass those specific markers. In a political context, this is further complicated by the risk of "censorship." If a platform moves too quickly to remove a suspected deepfake that turns out to be a real (but poorly shot) video, they face accusations of political interference. This hesitation creates a window of opportunity for malicious actors to exploit the "gray area" of content moderation during sensitive periods like election nights.

Broader Implications for Democracy

The long-term danger of political deepfakes is not just a single stolen election, but the total collapse of public trust in information. If any video can be faked, then any real video of corruption or misconduct can be dismissed as "AI-generated." This leads to a state of "epistemic fragmentation," where voters cannot agree on a basic set of facts required for healthy debate. For democracy to function, there must be an informed electorate; deepfakes threaten to replace that information with a permanent state of skepticism, where the loudest voice or the most convincing algorithm—rather than the truth—dictates the political landscape.
